ORIGINS OF THE VIRUS

First viruses were created for fun, or to prank someone. They were created for experimentation, and not to cause malicious intent.

The first virus was called the Creeper, created in 1971 by Bob Thomas at BBN Technologies, a research and development firm. It was an experimental self-replicating program that traveled across the internet, printing "I'm the Creeper, catch me if you can!" on infected machines. It was not malicious, but it created the idea for future viruses.

Although the first virus was created in 1971, the idea of self-replicating code was theorized much earlier. John von Neumann, a mathematician theorized the concept in the 1940s, with a paper being published in 1966. It followed 3 key principles: Damage Machines, Mass Replicate, and Infect

1971

THE CREEPER

EXPERIMENTAL

Created by Bob Thomas at BBN Technologies. Traveled across ARPANET printing "I'm the Creeper, catch me if you can!" The first self-replicating program — not malicious, but the ancestor of every virus that followed.

Damage: Minimal — Proof of concept

1974

RABBIT

SYSTEM KILLER

Also called Wabbit. Rapidly forked itself on UNIVAC systems, consuming all processing power until the machine crashed. One of the earliest denial-of-service attacks — against a single machine.

Damage: Local — System crashes

1982

ELK CLONER

FIRST PC VIRUS

Written by 15-year-old Rich Skrenta. Spread via floppy disks on Apple II machines. Every 50th boot, it displayed a poem. The first virus to spread "in the wild" — outside of a lab.

Damage: Low — Mostly annoying

1986

BRAIN

GLOBAL SPREAD

Created by brothers Basit and Amjad Farooq Alvi in Lahore, Pakistan to protect their medical software. It infected floppy disk boot sectors and spread globally — the first IBM PC virus. It even included their names, address, and phone number.

Damage: Moderate — 100,000+ disks infected
VARIANT INFECTIONS TRACKED382,000
1988

MORRIS WORM

INTERNET WORM

Released by Cornell grad student Robert Morris. Exploited Unix sendmail, fingerd, and rsh/rexec vulnerabilities. Infected ~6,000 machines — roughly 10% of the entire internet. First person convicted under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act.

Damage: $100M estimated — Internet slowdown
